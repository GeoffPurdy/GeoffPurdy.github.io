--- layout: post title: Model View Codeschool date: '2015-01-23T12:28:00.001-08:00' author: geoff purdy tags: modified_time: '2017-03-26T05:00:08.897-07:00' blogger_id: tag:blogger.com,1999:blog-1206336038417661147.post-1495547083538302626 blogger_orig_url: http://geoffreypurdy.blogspot.com/2015/01/model-view-codeschool.html --- What is code school anyway?

Is code school simply a professional service?  Code school will provide x hours of instruction and y hours of personal help and z hours of mentoring then work their network and find you a job?

Or, is it a College / University model in which, although one pays for the privilege of attending, the burden is on the student to meet the standards of the school or instructor?

Or, as suggested recently, is it an apprenticeship model?


Modern apprentices get paid for their services.  They never pay their master's salary.  Apprentices of old were often in involuntary servitude (let's not go there).  Maybe someday if people get paid to attend code school instead of paying for code school, that dynamic may change.

There are aspects of a College / University model in a code school.  There are standards to be met and a "graduation" of sorts upon completion.  But in my experience at a code school, any standards that exist are both unwritten and highly subjective.  That isn't the model in institutions of higher education.  And accredited institutions can confer degrees to students.  Code schools cannot do that.

That leaves us back where we started - the obvious and correct answer.  Code schools are professional service providers.  They advertise the services they provide and the cost of those services.  The students are the customers who, after paying the price should rightfully expect the services to be rendered as described.

That dynamic should be very straightforward, but it isn't.  When you go to code school, you plunk down five figures for the privilege of being there and for the next three months are necessarily and voluntarily unemployed.  I cannot speak for all code school attendees, but for the ones I know this translates into approaching the end of code school with no money and no job prospects other than your putative post-code-school career - the road to which travels through the good graces of code school staff.  At that point, the code school instructor and administration have a great deal of control of your future.  Not just your career, but the next time you can afford to eat or put fuel in your car.  And that can greatly distort the customer - vendor relationship.
