--- layout: post title: 'The Iron Yard: Escalator or Stepladder ' date: '2015-01-21T07:00:00.000-08:00' author: geoff purdy tags: modified_time: '2017-03-26T05:00:08.905-07:00' thumbnail: http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-kbfDMzQN4M4/VL-xRlSacsI/AAAAAAAABuw/rdvepiywXHU/s72-c/escalator.gif blogger_id: tag:blogger.com,1999:blog-1206336038417661147.post-2377646159481342439 blogger_orig_url: http://geoffreypurdy.blogspot.com/2015/01/escalator-or-stepladder.html --- My The Iron Yard cohort 2 colleagues have been on my mind a lot lately.  The Iron Yard's pitch is, among other things that you will leave a professional junior-level programmer.  However, instead of exiting into jr dev jobs, graduates seem to be fighting tooth and nail for internship level positions.  Jr dev jobs seem to be out of reach.

I think students entering the program, myself included, view the program as largely deterministic.  Come to class, pay attention, do the work, put in the 80+ hours a week and three months later you emerge a qualified jr. dev.  Like an escalator, you start at point A and time T later you exit at point B.  Of course, barring some failure on the part of the student like failing to attend class, failing to do the work or put in the hours.




In retrospect the expectations should be different.  One needs to evaluate their own starting point - their personal point A - and where they want to end up - personal point B.  And then figure out if three months at TIY will get them there.

For example, consider places you want to work.  Do they advertise internships for non-university students?  Do they have Jr. Dev positions advertised?  Is x years of experience or y degree a hard requirement to be considered?

In some cases, TIY may be just the ticket.  Maybe you are established in a company and want to transition to a developer role.  Maybe you are starting at ground zero and want to get an internship.  I think in these cases the finite boost provided by a code school may be just the right amount of education and demonstration of committment.






But the code school boost is finite and relative to point A.  Code school won't grant you a degree nor add years to your work history.  If graduating TIY isn't going to get you where you need to be to acheive that goal, then you need to find another path or re-evaluate your point B.  Sometimes a step ladder is the wrong tool for the job.



Finally, what does your local job market look like for devs in general?  If companies are fighting to attract and retain engineers, then they are likely to be more flexible in their hiring process and more receptive to code school grads.  If hiring is slow for devs and / or your region has a lot of experienced devs in the job market, then the bar is set that much higher for everyone.
